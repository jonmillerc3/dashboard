Analyst Narrative — Current Market Context
Supply Structure
Production is running at ~2.99M bbl/wk — up 16% vs 2023. But exports have absorbed nearly all that growth, running at 55–88% of production. The structural story: US propane is now a globally-traded commodity with domestic retail competing directly against Asian crackers and European heating demand.
Build Season Risk
The 2026-27 build season is tracking at only 6.3M bbls through week 9, versus a 5-year average of 15.4M — a 59% deficit. The elevated NS spread ($0.1225 vs $0.062 avg) tells you why: Gulf/export demand is pulling TET-basis barrels away from the Midwest. Conway is relatively cheap, but that discount hasn't been enough to attract storage builds at a normal pace. If this persists, a normal winter could create Midwest supply stress by February.
Hormuz Factor
WTI at $87/bbl is already pricing a geopolitical risk premium. The May 29 export drop to 1.65M bbls — lowest in recent weeks — may be the first data signal of tanker rerouting. Counterintuitively, Middle Eastern LPG disruption could increase demand for US exports, further pressuring domestic builds unless WTI-driven price signals incentivize storage over export.
Structural Forces Reshaping Global LPG Markets — 2026 and Beyond
⚠
Persian Gulf Infrastructure Damage
Feb–Apr 2026
The US-Israeli conflict with Iran beginning February 28, 2026 triggered a cascade of energy infrastructure damage across the Persian Gulf.

Ras Laffan, Qatar
Iranian missile strikes March 18–19 damaged LNG Trains 4 and 6, cutting Qatar's LNG export capacity by ~17% (12.8 MTPA). Qatar's LPG exports — ~13% of shipments — also impacted. Force majeure declared to buyers in China, South Korea, Italy and Belgium. Annual revenue loss: $20 billion.

Ju'aymah LPG Terminal, Saudi Arabia
Fires impacted LPG and NGL exports as of April 9. Saudi propane and butane exports halted for multiple weeks following earlier strikes on Ras Tanura.

South Pars / Asaluyeh, Iran
Israeli strikes March 18 damaged 12% of Iran's total gas production, halting two refineries with combined capacity of 100 million cubic meters per day.

Strait of Hormuz
Effectively closed through late May 2026. Iraq, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, UAE, Qatar and Bahrain collectively shut in 10.5 million barrels per day of crude oil production in April. Brent peaked at $138/bbl on April 7.
⏱
Recovery Timeline — Years, Not Months
Equipment constrained
The defining constraint on Persian Gulf LPG supply recovery is not capital — it is equipment lead times.

Ras Laffan: 3–5 years
Replacement gas turbines for refrigeration compressors face global delivery delays of 2–4 years. Only three manufacturers globally produce the large-frame turbines required. These suppliers entered 2026 with order books already stretched by data center expansion and coal plant retirements. Rystad Energy estimates total regional repair costs at $25+ billion.

Iran's South Pars
Iran's exclusion from Western supply chains means reliance on Chinese and domestic contractors — slower and more expensive. No credible timeline for full restoration.

Saudi facilities
Some may resume within months, but Ju'aymah LPG terminal damage timeline remains unclear.

The bottom line
Nearly half of Asia's propane imports in 2025 came from the Persian Gulf. That supply gap is structural — not temporary — and Asian buyers are actively rerouting to US supply for the medium term.
📈
US Production — Structurally Elevated
Record highs forecast
Approximately 70–80% of US propane production is derived from the natural gas processing stream. When raw natural gas is processed at gas plants, propane is extracted as a byproduct NGL before the methane moves on to pipelines or LNG export terminals. The remaining 20–30% comes from crude oil refining.

This means propane supply is a direct function of how much natural gas the US produces and processes — and that number is going structurally higher. The EIA forecasts US marketed natural gas production will reach record highs of 120.8 Bcf/d in 2026 and 122.3 Bcf/d in 2027, driven by the Permian, Haynesville and Appalachia regions.

The LNG Export Multiplier
Five new LNG export projects start through 2027, adding ~6 Bcf/d of capacity. Each Bcf/d of new LNG export capacity incentivizes additional upstream gas drilling. That incremental gas runs through processing plants where propane is extracted from the gas stream. More gas wells → more processing → more propane. The EIA forecasts propane production to grow 4% in 2026 and 6% in 2027.

These trends don't reverse quickly — the wells are drilled, the processing infrastructure is built, and the LNG export commitments are signed. Propane supply from the natural gas stream is effectively locked in at elevated levels for the foreseeable future.
🚢
VLGC Fleet — The Shipping Constraint
Why weekly data is noisy
Very Large Gas Carriers (VLGCs) are the sole vessels capable of moving propane economically on long-haul US-to-Asia routes. The global fleet is finite — approximately 370–390 active VLGCs worldwide, with an orderbook of ~27% of existing fleet delivering through 2027–28. Fleet growth is accelerating — 7% in 2026, peaking at 16% in 2027 — but new vessels take years to build and deploy.

Why your weekly EIA export number is inherently noisy
A standard VLGC carries approximately 500,000–550,000 barrels of propane per voyage. At current US export rates of ~12–15 VLGC loadings per week, a single vessel delayed by 2–3 days — berth congestion, weather, Panama Canal slot timing — moves the weekly EIA print by 500K+ barrels. Two ships slipping into the next week swings the number by over 1 million barrels with zero change in underlying demand.

The Cape of Good Hope factor
Panama Canal auction fees hit record highs in April 2026 — some slots reportedly fetching $4 million. A growing share of US-to-Asia cargoes now route via Cape of Good Hope instead, adding ~14 days to each voyage (30 days → 44 days). Longer voyages mean ships return to load less frequently, creating lumpier scheduling patterns and wider week-to-week export variance. Houston–Chiba VLGC freight surged ~55% month-over-month in April 2026, reaching near $290/metric ton.

The right way to read the data
The 4-week rolling average is the minimum smoothing window needed to filter vessel timing noise. Even that can be distorted if a cluster of ships all slipped the same week. The reliable signal is the 8–12 week trend direction — not any single weekly print. A one-week spike or drop almost always reflects ship scheduling, not a change in Asian demand for US propane.
US Export Infrastructure — Capacity Expanding Precisely When Asia Needs It New terminals online 2026
Enterprise Neches River Terminal
Shipped its first cargo in April 2026, ahead of its originally planned June launch. Phase 2 allows up to 360,000 bbl/day of propane loading capacity. The early startup directly captured diverted Asian demand as Hormuz closed.
Energy Transfer Nederland
250,000 bbl/day flex capacity brought online July 2025 has been fully deployed for LPG exports. Enterprise Houston terminal 300,000 bbl/day expansion expected later in 2026. Terminal fees surged from 8.6¢/gal in January to 44¢/gal by end of Q1 — a 5× increase reflecting tightening availability.
Global LPG trade rebalancing
Global seaborne LPG exports recovered to 4.8 million bbl/day in May 2026, led by the US. Supply remains ~600,000 bbl/day below February 2026 record highs. India, hit hardest by Persian Gulf supply loss, now relies heavily on US cargoes. The Mont Belvieu propane-ethane spread reached $325/ton on May 12, incentivizing propane over ethane at flexible terminals.
Strategic Implications for Retail Propane Markets The retail takeaway
Export Demand Pull
Asian buyers replacing Persian Gulf supply with US propane for the next 3–5 years. This structurally competes with domestic storage builds — every barrel exported is a barrel not in a tank in Kansas or Iowa. The weak 2026-27 build season pace (59% below 5-year average) is partly a direct consequence.
Production Baseline
Record US gas production means propane supply is not going to tighten from the production side. The only mechanism that tightens domestic supply is exports absorbing an increasing share — which is exactly what is happening and will continue for years. High domestic inventory is the buffer, but it is being drawn down faster than normal build rates replenish it.
The Forward Buying Window
Record inventory and C3:WTI ratios at the 5th percentile of their seasonal range create a historically rare forward buying opportunity. But the export structural shift means this window is finite. As Persian Gulf supply stays offline for years, sustained Asian demand for US propane will keep pressure on domestic builds — and the window between spot value and forward risk may narrow sooner than a pure inventory analysis suggests.
